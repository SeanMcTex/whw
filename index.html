Long Distance
📋 Training Plan
Key things you can't train for in Texas: boggy ground, wet rock, stream crossings, and 7 consecutive days. For those, load your pack to 15–20 lbs and do long flat walks — that teaches you more about your boots and pack than any hill session. Break in your boots NOW. 8–12 weeks of active use before departure.
Boots — when to buy & break in
The sweet spot
Buy now (5 months out) — ideal timing. 8–12 weeks of active use is the break-in target. Wear them on every training walk from day one; don't save them. By 4–6 weeks out they should feel like old friends. If they still don't fit well at that point, you still have time to switch.
Don't worry about wearing them out
Quality hiking boots last 500–1,000 miles. Your break-in adds maybe 50–100 miles — a rounding error on their lifespan. The real danger is buying too close to departure, not getting enough wear on them, and destroying your feet on Day 1.
What to look for
Waterproof (Gore-Tex or equivalent, not just water-resistant — the distinction matters enormously in October Highland bog). Ankle support. A genuine hiking boot, not a trail runner. Pair with merino wool socks; use the same socks during break-in as you'll wear on the trail.
Alternative: trail running shoes
Sharky's colleague Chris Newton completed the WHW in breathable trail runners with a rock plate and preferred them to boots. His reasoning: only some sections are rocky enough to need ankle support, your feet will get wet regardless (from sweat, rain, and streams), and trail runners dry out fast where waterproof boots stay soggy. Mac's still recommends waterproof boots for October conditions. Both approaches work — the right call depends on your feet, pack weight, and comfort with rocky terrain. If you go the trail runner route, bring extra wool socks and prioritize a rock plate for underfoot protection.
October conditions
Rain and mud are near-certain. Rannoch Moor on Day 5 is a legitimate bog. Waterproofness is not optional — if your boots get wet inside, you'll have blisters within hours. Gaiters help too.
Your feet will get wet either way
Between sweat, rain, and stream crossings, wet feet on the WHW are basically a given. Chris Newton's advice: bring plenty of merino wool socks and change into dry ones mid-day if needed. (He used Smartwool and stayed warm and blister-free the whole trip.) The Scottish tap water is excellent — every hotel and outside spigot he tried tasted better than filtered water at home, so don't overthink water treatment.
Training
The real challenge
Not any single day — it's the cumulative multi-day fatigue. Training with back-to-back long walks on weekends is the single most useful thing you can do. Your legs on Day 5 will feel very different from your legs on Day 1.
Mileage targets
Now–June: 5–8 miles, 3x/week. July: 10–12 miles long day. August: 15–18 miles long day + 2–3 day back-to-back hike. Your hardest day on the itinerary is 18.5 miles (Day 5) — you want to have exceeded that in training before you leave.
Most of us are training flat — address that
The WHW has ~3,500m total elevation gain. Only Ross has real mountains nearby — the rest of us need to actively seek out hills in training, or supplement with squats, lunges, calf raises, and step-ups. The climbs (Conic Hill, Devil's Staircase, Mamore Ridge) will bite if your legs aren't prepared for ascent and descent. See the Training tab for location-specific hikes.
Train in bad weather
Don't skip rainy days. You need to know your gear works, and you need to be mentally comfortable walking in driving Scottish rain. Get comfortable with discomfort before Scotland.
Taper
2–3 weeks before departure, reduce big mileage. Arrive rested, not worn out. A final 12–15 mile confidence walk in mid-September, then coast in.

From the trail — colleague tips
Day pack: less is more
Minimize day pack weight — it feels heavier every mile. Chris Newton overpacked with first aid kit, backup battery, water filter, etc. and never used any of it. Stick to: extra socks, layers, rain gear, snacks, water, blister kit. Hotels often have a water spigot for refilling, so you don't need to carry a full day's supply.
Leave room in your transferred bag
Chris Newton's tip: leave a little extra space in your main luggage (the one AMS transfers between stops). If you decide mid-hike that you don't need something in your day pack — a layer, a battery pack, whatever — you can offload it into your main bag that evening and not carry it the next day. A packed-tight transfer bag leaves you no flexibility.
Blister care: don't wait
Carry Compeed (not just moleskin) and don't ignore any hot spots or rubbing — deal with them immediately, not "at the next stop." A small rub becomes a trip-ending blister in hours on a thru-hike.
Body Glide: use it everywhere
Unless you're used to hiking this much for this long, apply Body Glide wherever skin rubs skin — especially the groin. This matters even with good clothing. They make it for feet too. If you do get a raw spot, clean it well and apply Aquaphor at bedtime — Chris Newton went to sleep with terrible chafing and woke up completely healed. Epsom salt soak if your room has a tub is a superpower for recovery.
Rain strategies
A good rain jacket + rain pants works double duty for wind and rain. Use a pack liner (a plastic bag inside your pack like a trash bag) or a pack cover. Oversized ponchos that cover you and your pack are also smart — and double as privacy for trail bathroom breaks. Bring a small clothesline to string across your room at night; some places have drying rooms but some don't.
Midges
October is late enough that midges shouldn't be a major issue, but bring a headnet rated for midges (not just mosquitoes) — look for "ultra-fine" mesh. Bug spray is available at most trail stops if they turn out to be bad. We may not see them at all in October, but better to have the net and not need it.
Walking poles: non-negotiable?
Chris Newton says trekking poles were non-negotiable for him — he would not do the WHW without them. Mac's recommends them as optional but strongly encouraged. They reduce knee strain on descents (Devil's Staircase, Mamore Ridge), help with stream crossings, and save your legs over a full week. Note: you can only pack poles in checked luggage, not carry-on.
Dinner reservations matter
Some trail towns have very few dining options. Plan ahead and book dinner at your overnight stop when you can — don't assume you'll find a table. This is especially important in the smaller villages like Rowardennan and Kinlochleven.
Packed lunches from your B&B
Most hotels and B&Bs will make you a packed lunch if you ask — sandwiches, chips, fruit, a drink, sometimes a chocolate bar. Ask reception before 6–7pm the night before. This saves carrying a day's worth of food and is often better than what you'd find on trail.
Electrolytes every evening
Chris Newton recommends electrolyte packets at the end of each day's hike. He believes it made a real difference in reducing cramps and dehydration headaches. Lightweight, easy to pack, easy to overlook — but worth it on a multi-day effort.
Carry small bills for honesty boxes
You'll encounter random honesty boxes and small cafes along the trail. Having a little extra cash in small denominations (£1, £5) is handy for these. Some stops only take cash.
The community is the best part
Chris Newton didn't expect this, but the WHW is a shared experience with the people you end up hiking near every day. Passing the same people on trail, sharing a pint at the end of the day, everyone arriving in each new town — it feels like a pilgrimage. Make sure your group is on the same page with gear and pacing so no one has to miss a day.
Customs: wash your hiking shoes before flying home
Scotland is farmland — you'll be walking through fields and past livestock. When you fill out the customs declaration ("Have you been on a farm or near farm animals?"), the honest answer is yes. Chris Newton answered honestly but hadn't washed his boots and got sent to secondary screening. Wash your hiking shoes before packing them for the return flight so you can truthfully say yes without the extra hassle.
Travel insurance
Required by Mac's Adventure
It is a condition of booking that you have suitable travel insurance covering the activity, emergency evacuation, and hospital care. Mac's recommends Campbell Irvine Ltd — however, their policies are only available to UK residents (3+ months residency required), so they won't work for us.
What your policy must cover
Emergency medical — hospital care in Scotland. Emergency evacuation — rescue off the trail (helicopter, ambulance). Repatriation — flying you home if medically necessary (can be $10K–$50K+). Trip cancellation/interruption — recovering your Mac's booking costs if you can't travel or continue. Baggage loss/delay — gear doesn't make it to Glasgow.
WHW altitude — you're just hill-walking
The highest point on the WHW is Devil's Staircase at 550m (1,804 ft). Conic Hill is 358m. Most of the route is 50–400m. This is well under the 2,500m threshold where "trekking" coverage kicks in — any standard policy covering "hiking" or "walking" will include the WHW with no altitude add-on needed.
Your regular health insurance (US plans)
Most US health plans have limited or no coverage outside the US. Even if emergency care abroad is covered, you'd likely pay out-of-network rates and file claims later. US health insurance does not cover emergency evacuation, repatriation, trip cancellation, or baggage loss. Travel insurance is primary for anything that happens in Scotland — file with them first. Check with your health insurer to confirm what (if any) international emergency coverage your plan includes, but you still need separate travel insurance regardless.
